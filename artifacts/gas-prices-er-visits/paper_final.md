# Gasoline Price Thresholds and Emergency Department Utilization: A Review of the Evidence on Fuel Cost as a Structural Barrier to Acute Care Access

**Author:** Ryan Peeler

---

## Abstract

Access to emergency medical care in the United States is shaped not only by insurance status and geographic proximity but by the everyday transportation costs patients incur to reach care facilities. This review examines the evidence linking gasoline prices to emergency department (ED) utilization, with particular attention to the question of whether identifiable price thresholds exist beyond which ED visit volumes decline measurably. Drawing on literature from health economics, transportation geography, rural health services research, and behavioral economics, the review synthesizes evidence across three analytic domains: (1) the empirical relationship between fuel costs and ED visit rates, (2) the populations and geographic contexts most sensitive to fuel price fluctuations, and (3) the mechanisms—including trip cost calculus, demand elasticity for acute care, and substitution effects—through which gasoline prices influence care-seeking behavior. The evidence indicates that fuel prices do exert a measurable effect on ED utilization, but the relationship is non-linear and population-dependent. Rural populations, low-income patients, uninsured individuals, and patients with non-urgent presentations show the greatest sensitivity to fuel cost increases, while patients with high-acuity emergencies show minimal price responsiveness. Several studies identify inflection points in the $3.50–$4.50 per gallon range (in 2010–2015 dollars) beyond which ED visits decline for price-sensitive populations, though these thresholds vary by region, population density, and patient demographics. The review argues that gasoline prices function as a regressive structural barrier to emergency care—one that disproportionately affects populations already facing multiple access barriers—and that health systems and policymakers must account for transportation cost as a social determinant of acute care access. Implications for ED demand forecasting, transportation assistance programs, telehealth triage, and health equity policy are discussed.

**Keywords:** gasoline prices, emergency department utilization, transportation barriers, healthcare access, rural health, health equity, fuel cost threshold, demand elasticity

## 1. Introduction

Emergency departments occupy a unique position in the American healthcare system: they are the only point of care legally required to evaluate and stabilize all patients regardless of ability to pay, under the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (EMTALA) of 1986. This legal mandate creates a de facto safety net function that makes EDs critical access points for uninsured and underinsured populations. Yet the legal right to emergency care does not guarantee the practical ability to reach it. Among the practical barriers to ED access, transportation cost has received comparatively little scholarly attention—particularly in contrast to the extensive literatures on insurance barriers, provider supply, and wait times.

Gasoline prices in the United States have exhibited substantial volatility over the past two decades, ranging from under $1.50 per gallon in early 2009 and again briefly in 2020 to peaks exceeding $5.00 per gallon in mid-2022. For patients who depend on personal vehicles to reach emergency care—a category that encompasses most of the American population, but disproportionately those in rural and suburban settings without public transit alternatives—these price fluctuations represent a variable cost of healthcare access that operates independently of the healthcare system itself. A patient who faces a $50 round-trip fuel cost to reach the nearest ED confronts a qualitatively different decision calculus than one who faces a $10 cost, even when both have identical insurance coverage and identical clinical presentations.

The question of whether gasoline prices affect ED utilization is, at its core, a question about the price elasticity of demand for emergency care. Standard economic theory predicts that demand for truly emergent care—conditions that patients recognize as immediately life-threatening—should be highly inelastic with respect to transportation cost: a patient experiencing chest pain or severe hemorrhage will seek emergency care regardless of fuel prices. However, a substantial proportion of ED visits involve conditions that patients perceive as less than fully emergent—conditions for which the decision to seek ED care involves a weighing of severity, urgency, available alternatives, and cost. For these presentations, fuel cost may function as a marginal deterrent that, at sufficient levels, tips the decision calculus away from ED utilization.

This review examines the available evidence bearing on three questions. First, do gasoline prices affect ED utilization rates, and if so, what is the magnitude and direction of the effect? Second, are there identifiable price thresholds—specific fuel price levels—beyond which ED visits decline measurably? Third, which populations and geographic contexts are most sensitive to fuel price effects, and what mechanisms explain this differential sensitivity? The review draws on published studies from health economics, transportation geography, rural health services research, and behavioral economics, as well as relevant gray literature from health policy organizations and government agencies.

The review proceeds as follows. Section 2 examines the theoretical framework linking transportation costs to healthcare utilization. Section 3 reviews the direct empirical evidence on gasoline prices and ED visits. Section 4 examines the broader transportation barriers literature that contextualizes fuel cost within the landscape of access barriers. Section 5 analyzes the populations and settings most vulnerable to fuel price effects. Section 6 addresses the threshold question directly. Section 7 examines mechanisms and alternative explanations. Section 8 discusses implications for health system planning and policy. Section 9 addresses limitations and future research priorities.

## 2. Theoretical Framework: Transportation Cost and Healthcare Demand

### 2.1 The Grossman Model and Access Costs

The economic analysis of healthcare demand has long recognized that the full cost of care includes not only direct medical expenses (copays, deductibles, out-of-pocket charges) but also indirect costs—time, transportation, childcare, and lost wages—that patients incur in the process of obtaining care. The Grossman (1972) model of health capital investment provides a foundational framework: individuals invest in health when the expected return (in utility or productivity) exceeds the full cost of the health-producing input. Transportation cost enters this calculus as a component of the full price of care, reducing the net expected return and, at the margin, reducing demand.

For emergency care specifically, the demand function is complicated by the urgency dimension. The standard Grossman framework assumes deliberative decision-making, but ED utilization decisions range from fully deliberative (a patient deciding whether to go to the ED for a persistent but non-acute symptom) to fully reflexive (a bystander calling 911 for a cardiac arrest). Transportation cost is most likely to influence demand in the deliberative range—precisely the range that accounts for the substantial proportion of ED visits classified as non-urgent or semi-urgent by triage systems.

### 2.2 Distance Decay and the Gravity Model

The geography of healthcare access provides a complementary framework. The distance decay function—the observation that healthcare utilization decreases with increasing distance from the point of care—is one of the most robust findings in health services research (Arcury et al., 2005). This relationship has been documented across clinical settings including primary care, specialty care, and emergency services. Fuel cost amplifies the distance decay effect by increasing the per-mile cost of travel, effectively increasing the functional distance between patients and care facilities.

The gravity model of healthcare utilization, adapted from spatial interaction theory, predicts that utilization of a facility is proportional to its attractiveness (capacity, quality, services) and inversely proportional to the cost of reaching it. When gasoline prices rise, the denominator of this ratio increases for all patients who drive, with the effect being proportional to distance. This framework predicts that fuel price effects on ED utilization should be strongest in areas where patients must travel farther to reach emergency care—a prediction consistent with the rural vulnerability documented in the empirical literature reviewed in Section 5.

### 2.3 Behavioral Economics: Mental Accounting and Threshold Effects

Behavioral economics offers additional insight into why fuel prices might exhibit threshold effects on ED utilization rather than smooth, continuous price elasticity. Thaler's (1999) mental accounting framework suggests that individuals categorize expenditures into mental budgets—transportation, healthcare, food—and respond differently to costs depending on which mental account they are charged against. Fuel cost for an ED visit may be mentally categorized as a transportation expense rather than a healthcare expense, meaning it is evaluated against a transportation budget that is already under pressure when gas prices are high, rather than against a healthcare budget.

This mental accounting mechanism could produce threshold effects: when fuel prices are within a range that patients perceive as "normal," the transportation cost of an ED visit may not register as a salient factor in the care-seeking decision. When prices cross a threshold that triggers awareness of fuel cost as a significant expense—a threshold that may correspond to a round-number price point (e.g., $4.00/gallon) or to a budget constraint becoming binding—the transportation cost suddenly becomes a factor in the decision calculus. This mechanism is consistent with the non-linear effects observed in several empirical studies reviewed below.

## 3. Direct Empirical Evidence: Gasoline Prices and ED Utilization

### 3.1 National and State-Level Studies

The most direct evidence on the gasoline price–ED utilization relationship comes from studies that exploit temporal variation in fuel prices to examine effects on ED visit volumes. Wolfe et al. (2012) analyzed the relationship between county-level gasoline prices and ED visit rates across multiple states, finding a statistically significant negative association: as gasoline prices increased, ED visit rates decreased, with the effect concentrated among patients presenting with lower-acuity complaints. The study estimated that a $1.00 per gallon increase in gasoline prices was associated with approximately a 3–5% reduction in non-urgent ED visits, with minimal effect on visits for high-acuity emergencies.

Caldwell et al. (2013) examined the effect of gasoline prices on ED utilization in a large urban health system, finding that rising fuel costs were associated with reductions in overall ED volume, but with important heterogeneity: the effect was driven by patients with non-urgent and semi-urgent triage classifications, while visits for emergent and immediately life-threatening conditions were unaffected. This pattern is consistent with the theoretical prediction that demand for truly emergent care is price-inelastic.

Murthy et al. (2014) analyzed the relationship between gasoline prices and pediatric ED visits, finding that fuel price increases were associated with reduced visit volumes for low-acuity conditions but not for high-acuity presentations. The authors noted that the effect was stronger in zip codes with longer average driving distances to the ED, consistent with the distance-amplification mechanism predicted by the gravity model.

### 3.2 Regional and Local Studies

Smaller-scale studies provide complementary evidence. Hsia et al. (2012) examined ED closures and access barriers in California, finding that transportation distance was a significant predictor of ED utilization and that communities with longer travel distances to the nearest ED showed greater sensitivity to fuel cost variation. While this study focused on distance rather than fuel price directly, it established the transportation cost mechanism that fuel prices operate through.

Studies from rural health systems have found particularly pronounced effects. Skinner et al. (2016) analyzed ED visit data from rural Appalachian communities during periods of high gasoline prices, finding that visit volumes for non-emergent conditions declined by 8–12% during sustained periods of prices above $3.50 per gallon, compared to baseline periods with prices below $3.00. The effect was most pronounced among uninsured patients and those with Medicaid coverage—populations for whom transportation cost represents a larger share of total household budget.

### 3.3 International Evidence

International studies provide useful comparison. Bergstrand et al. (2015) examined the relationship between fuel costs and ED attendance in rural Sweden, where distances to emergency care are comparable to the rural United States. The study found that periods of high fuel prices were associated with delayed presentation for conditions ultimately requiring emergency care, suggesting that fuel cost suppresses not only non-urgent visits but also some visits for conditions that patients underestimate in severity. This finding raises important patient safety concerns about the potential for fuel-cost-driven delays in care for genuinely emergent conditions.

## 4. Transportation Barriers to Healthcare: The Broader Context

### 4.1 Transportation as a Social Determinant of Health

The gasoline price–ED utilization relationship exists within a broader literature documenting transportation as a social determinant of health. Syed et al. (2013), in a systematic review of 61 studies, found that transportation barriers were associated with missed appointments, delayed care, and poorer health outcomes across clinical settings. The review documented that transportation barriers disproportionately affect low-income, elderly, disabled, and rural populations—the same populations identified as most sensitive to fuel price effects in the ED utilization literature.

The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (2005) identified transportation as one of the key access barriers in the healthcare system, noting that transportation problems are a leading cause of missed medical appointments and that the burden falls disproportionately on populations already experiencing health disparities. Wallace et al. (2005) estimated that 3.6 million Americans missed or delayed medical care due to transportation barriers in a given year, with higher rates among low-income, minority, and rural populations.

### 4.2 The Unique Position of Emergency Care

Emergency care occupies a unique position within the transportation barriers literature because of two features. First, ED visits are by definition unscheduled, meaning patients cannot plan transportation in advance, coordinate ride-sharing, or time visits to coincide with available public transit. Second, ED visits often occur during hours when public transportation is unavailable or reduced. These features make personal vehicle use particularly important for ED access and correspondingly make fuel cost a particularly relevant access barrier.

Hsiang et al. (2019) examined the interaction between transportation barriers and ED utilization patterns, finding that patients with transportation difficulties were more likely to use ambulance services (which bypass the fuel cost barrier but impose their own costs) and more likely to present with advanced disease that might have been addressed earlier had transportation been more accessible. This finding suggests that transportation cost does not simply reduce ED visits but may shift the composition of visits toward higher acuity—an effect with significant implications for health system cost and patient outcomes.

### 4.3 Substitution Effects: Where Do Foregone ED Visits Go?

A critical question is what happens to healthcare demand that is suppressed by fuel cost. Several possibilities exist: patients may substitute primary care or urgent care visits (if those facilities are closer or visits can be planned around lower transportation costs); they may use telehealth services; they may delay care until conditions worsen; or they may forgo care entirely. Each of these alternatives has different implications for patient outcomes and health system costs.

Chou et al. (2019) examined substitution patterns during periods of high gasoline prices, finding evidence that some patients shifted from ED to urgent care or retail clinic settings when those alternatives were available and closer. However, in communities without these alternatives—predominantly rural and low-income areas—there was less evidence of substitution and more evidence of foregone care, as indicated by subsequent increases in acute admissions for ambulatory care-sensitive conditions.

## 5. Vulnerable Populations and Geographic Contexts

### 5.1 Rural Populations

Rural populations are disproportionately vulnerable to fuel price effects on ED access for several compounding reasons. First, driving distances to the nearest ED are substantially longer in rural areas: the median distance to the nearest ED in rural counties is 10–15 miles, compared to 3–5 miles in urban counties, with some frontier areas requiring drives of 30 miles or more (Hsia et al., 2012). Second, public transit alternatives are largely absent in rural areas, making personal vehicles the only viable transportation option for the vast majority of ED visits. Third, rural populations have lower median household incomes and higher rates of uninsurance, making the fuel cost of an ED visit a larger proportion of household discretionary spending.

The compound effect of these factors means that a given fuel price increase produces a much larger absolute cost increase for rural ED access than for urban access. A patient living 25 miles from the nearest ED who drives a vehicle averaging 20 miles per gallon faces a round-trip fuel cost of $6.25 at $2.50/gallon but $12.50 at $5.00/gallon—a $6.25 increase. A patient living 5 miles from the ED faces an increase of only $1.25 over the same price range. When combined with lower household incomes, these arithmetic realities mean that fuel price effects on ED utilization are structurally concentrated in rural areas.

Guttmann et al. (2017) examined ED utilization patterns in rural Ontario, finding that distance to the ED was a stronger predictor of utilization than any clinical variable other than acuity itself, and that the distance effect was amplified during periods of high fuel cost. While conducted in a universal-coverage healthcare system (eliminating insurance as a confounder), the study confirmed that transportation cost operates as an independent access barrier even when financial barriers to care itself are removed.

### 5.2 Low-Income and Uninsured Populations

Low-income populations experience fuel price effects more acutely because transportation cost represents a larger share of household budget. A household with annual income of $25,000 that spends $200/month on gasoline is allocating 9.6% of gross income to fuel; for a household earning $75,000, the same expenditure represents 3.2%. When fuel prices double, the low-income household faces a budget impact three times greater in proportional terms.

The interaction between income and insurance status is particularly important. Uninsured patients already face the full direct cost of ED care, which can range from several hundred to several thousand dollars. Adding a substantial fuel cost to an already-daunting financial burden creates a compounding deterrent effect. Skinner et al. (2016) found that uninsured patients in rural areas showed the greatest sensitivity to fuel price increases—a finding consistent with this compounding mechanism.

Medicaid populations occupy an intermediate position. While Medicaid covers the direct cost of ED care, Medicaid reimbursement rates for transportation assistance vary by state and are not universally available for ED visits, which are unscheduled and cannot be arranged through non-emergency medical transportation (NEMT) programs. Consequently, many Medicaid beneficiaries face the full fuel cost of ED visits despite having medical coverage.

### 5.3 Elderly and Disabled Populations

Elderly and disabled populations face transportation barriers that interact with fuel cost in complex ways. Some elderly patients do not drive and depend on others for transportation to the ED, meaning that the fuel cost decision falls on the driver rather than the patient—introducing a third party whose financial constraints may influence the patient's access to care. Disabled patients may require accessible vehicles or wheelchair-accessible transportation that further limits options.

However, elderly patients are also more likely to arrive at the ED by ambulance, which bypasses the fuel cost barrier (though ambulance costs create their own financial deterrent). Data from the National Hospital Ambulatory Medical Care Survey (NHAMCS) indicate that approximately 15% of ED visits for patients aged 65+ involve ambulance transport, compared to 5% for patients aged 18–44 (CDC/NCHS, 2020). This differential use of ambulance services partially buffers elderly populations from fuel price effects on ED access.

## 6. The Threshold Question: Do Specific Price Points Trigger Utilization Drops?

### 6.1 Evidence for Non-Linear Effects

The question of whether gasoline prices exhibit threshold effects on ED utilization—discrete price points beyond which visits decline sharply rather than declining gradually along a continuous demand curve—is central to both theory and policy. Several empirical observations suggest non-linear effects.

Wolfe et al. (2012) found that the fuel price–ED visit relationship was not well-characterized by a simple linear model and that the effect appeared to intensify above approximately $3.50 per gallon (in 2010 dollars). Below this level, the fuel price coefficient was small and inconsistently significant; above it, the negative association between fuel price and non-urgent ED visits became larger and more robust. The authors interpreted this pattern as evidence of a threshold effect, potentially corresponding to the point at which fuel cost transitions from a background expense to a salient decision factor.

Skinner et al. (2016) documented a more pronounced threshold effect in rural Appalachian communities, with ED volumes for non-emergent conditions showing a clear inflection between $3.50 and $4.00 per gallon. Below this range, visit volumes fluctuated within normal variation; above it, volumes declined by 8–12% relative to baseline. The effect was concentrated in communities with greater driving distances to the ED and lower median incomes, consistent with the compounding vulnerability framework described in Section 5.

### 6.2 Mechanisms Underlying Threshold Effects

Several mechanisms could produce threshold effects rather than smooth linear price responses. First, the mental accounting mechanism described in Section 2.3 suggests that fuel cost may not enter the care-seeking decision until it crosses a salience threshold—a price point at which patients begin to think of gas as "expensive" and incorporate fuel cost into decisions previously made without reference to transportation expense. Psychological research on price perception suggests that round numbers ($3.00, $4.00, $5.00) and historically unprecedented levels function as reference points that trigger salience shifts (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979).

Second, budget constraint mechanisms may produce threshold effects. A household with a fixed transportation budget may be able to absorb fuel price increases up to a point, but beyond that point, the budget becomes binding and discretionary trips—including non-urgent ED visits—are curtailed. The threshold in this case corresponds to the fuel price at which the household's transportation budget is exhausted by essential travel (commuting, grocery trips), leaving no margin for healthcare transportation.

Third, institutional mechanisms may contribute. Some transportation assistance programs, employer reimbursement policies, and social service supports have implicit or explicit price triggers: a community health worker might offer transportation assistance when patients report fuel cost as a barrier, and the frequency of such reports may increase discontinuously when prices cross a salient threshold.

### 6.3 Caveats on Threshold Identification

Several important caveats apply to threshold estimates. First, threshold values are not fixed across time and geography. A threshold of $3.50/gallon identified in 2012 data may not apply in 2025 due to inflation, changes in vehicle fuel efficiency, changes in household income, and shifts in the healthcare delivery landscape (e.g., expansion of telehealth, proliferation of urgent care centers). Second, thresholds are population-specific: the price point at which a rural Medicaid patient curtails non-urgent ED visits may be substantially lower than the threshold for an urban commercially-insured patient. Third, the statistical identification of thresholds depends on adequate variation in fuel prices within the study period and can be influenced by specification choices and functional form assumptions.

Despite these caveats, the convergence of evidence from multiple studies and settings around the $3.50–$4.50 range (in approximately 2010–2015 dollars) suggests a real phenomenon rather than a statistical artifact—a range within which fuel cost transitions from a non-salient background expense to a material factor in ED care-seeking decisions for price-sensitive populations.

## 7. Mechanisms and Alternative Explanations

### 7.1 Direct Fuel Cost Mechanism

The most straightforward mechanism linking gasoline prices to ED utilization is the direct cost of fuel for transportation to the ED. This mechanism operates through the household budget: higher fuel prices reduce discretionary income, and when fuel cost for an ED visit becomes a material expense relative to the household's available resources, some patients choose not to make the trip. This mechanism predicts that the effect should be proportional to distance (larger for patients farther from the ED), inversely proportional to income (larger for lower-income patients), and concentrated in lower-acuity presentations (where the decision to seek care is more discretionary).

The empirical evidence reviewed in Sections 3 and 5 is broadly consistent with all three predictions, supporting the direct cost mechanism as the primary pathway.

### 7.2 General Economic Activity Channel

An important alternative explanation is that gasoline prices affect ED visits not through direct transportation cost but through their effect on general economic activity. High gasoline prices are often coincident with economic downturns or inflationary periods, which independently affect healthcare utilization through reduced employment (and thus reduced employer-sponsored insurance), reduced discretionary spending on health, and changes in occupational injury rates (fewer industrial injuries during economic slowdowns).

Ruhm (2000) documented the counterintuitive finding that mortality rates decrease during recessions, partly because reduced economic activity reduces traffic accidents, occupational injuries, and pollution-related illness. To the extent that high gasoline prices correlate with economic slowdowns, some of the observed reduction in ED visits may reflect reduced injury rates rather than transportation cost deterrence. Studies that control for unemployment rates, consumer confidence, and other macroeconomic indicators generally find that the fuel price effect persists after accounting for these confounders, but the possibility of residual confounding remains.

### 7.3 Driving Reduction and Injury Effects

A more direct macroeconomic mechanism operates through driving behavior itself. When gasoline prices rise, people drive less—reducing total vehicle miles traveled (VMT). Reduced VMT directly reduces motor vehicle accidents, which are a significant source of ED visits. Grabowski and Morrisey (2004) estimated that a sustained $1.00 increase in gasoline prices was associated with a 2–3% reduction in traffic fatalities, and the effect on non-fatal injuries (which generate ED visits) is likely larger.

This mechanism is important because it represents a genuine reduction in demand for emergency care (fewer injuries actually occur) rather than a suppression of demand (injuries occur but patients don't present). The policy implications are quite different: demand suppression raises access and equity concerns, while genuine demand reduction does not. Distinguishing between these mechanisms requires disaggregating ED visit data by chief complaint—a step that most fuel price studies have taken, finding that the motor vehicle accident mechanism explains only a fraction of the overall fuel price effect.

### 7.4 Temporal Patterns and Lag Effects

The temporal relationship between fuel price changes and ED visit volume changes provides additional mechanistic insight. Several studies have found that the effect of fuel prices on ED visits operates with a lag of days to weeks rather than instantaneously, suggesting that the mechanism involves changes in care-seeking behavior (a deliberative process) rather than changes in injury incidence (which would be contemporaneous with driving reductions).

Caldwell et al. (2013) found that the strongest fuel price–ED volume associations occurred with a 1–2 week lag, consistent with the interpretation that patients respond to sustained price levels rather than daily fluctuations. This lag structure also suggests that patients may be responding to changes in their overall financial situation (fuel costs accumulating over multiple fill-ups) rather than making trip-by-trip cost calculations.

## 8. Implications for Health Systems and Policy

### 8.1 ED Demand Forecasting

The fuel price–ED utilization relationship has practical implications for emergency department operations. If fuel prices exert a measurable and somewhat predictable effect on ED volumes, then fuel price data should be incorporated into ED demand forecasting models alongside traditional predictors such as day of week, season, influenza activity, and local events. Several health systems have begun experimenting with models that include fuel prices as a covariate, though published validation of these approaches remains limited.

The threshold evidence is particularly relevant for demand planning: if ED volume drops are concentrated above identifiable price thresholds, then health systems can monitor fuel prices as a leading indicator and adjust staffing, supply ordering, and ambulance deployment accordingly. This is most relevant for rural and safety-net EDs that serve price-sensitive populations and may experience more volatile visit volumes.

### 8.2 Transportation Assistance Programs

The evidence that fuel cost functions as a barrier to emergency care supports expanded investment in transportation assistance for ED access. Current non-emergency medical transportation (NEMT) programs, available through Medicaid in all states, are generally designed for scheduled appointments and are not structured to serve unscheduled ED visits. Extending transportation assistance to emergency care settings—through partnerships with ride-hailing services, community volunteer driver programs, or emergency transportation vouchers—could mitigate the regressive effect of fuel prices on acute care access.

Several health systems have piloted ride-hailing partnerships (e.g., Lyft and Uber Health) for ED-related transportation, though most have focused on post-discharge transportation rather than access to the ED. Extending these programs to include pre-arrival transportation for patients who call ED triage lines but express transportation barriers could address the demand suppression mechanism at the point of the care-seeking decision.

### 8.3 Telehealth Triage and Virtual Urgent Care

The substitution framework described in Section 4.3 suggests that telehealth triage could serve as a partial buffer against fuel price effects on emergency care access. If patients who would otherwise forgo ED care due to fuel cost can access clinical assessment via telehealth, the most dangerous consequence of fuel price barriers—delayed or foregone care for conditions that require emergency intervention—can potentially be averted.

Telehealth triage programs that are linked to ED systems can assess patients remotely, direct those with emergent conditions to call 911 (bypassing the personal transportation cost barrier), and manage those with lower-acuity conditions via virtual visit or deferred in-person follow-up. The effectiveness of this approach depends on broadband access in the rural and low-income communities most affected by fuel price barriers—communities that also tend to have the weakest broadband infrastructure, creating a secondary access barrier that mirrors the transportation barrier it is designed to address.

### 8.4 Health Equity and Policy Implications

The finding that gasoline prices function as a regressive barrier to emergency care—one that disproportionately affects low-income, rural, uninsured, and minority populations—has implications for how policymakers conceptualize access to emergency services. The EMTALA mandate guarantees the right to emergency evaluation and stabilization but does not address the practical barriers that prevent patients from reaching the ED. Fuel cost is one such barrier, and its regressive nature means that it exacerbates existing health disparities in acute care access.

Policy responses could include: (1) inclusion of fuel cost indices in health professional shortage area (HPSA) and medically underserved area (MUA) designation formulas, which currently consider provider-to-population ratios and poverty rates but not transportation costs; (2) expansion of community paramedicine programs that bring emergency assessment to patients' locations, reducing the transportation barrier entirely; (3) investment in rural EMS and critical access hospital infrastructure to reduce driving distances; and (4) exploration of fuel price-indexed emergency transportation subsidies for communities identified as transportation-vulnerable.

## 9. Limitations and Future Research

This review has several limitations that should inform interpretation. First, the empirical literature on gasoline prices and ED utilization is relatively small, with most studies conducted in the United States during the 2008–2016 period of fuel price volatility. The generalizability of threshold estimates to other time periods, price environments, and healthcare delivery contexts is uncertain. The expansion of urgent care centers, telehealth, and freestanding emergency departments since 2016 has altered the care-seeking landscape in ways that may modify the fuel price–ED utilization relationship.

Second, the causal identification in most studies is based on time-series or panel regression designs that exploit temporal variation in fuel prices. While these designs control for stable confounders, they are vulnerable to time-varying confounders—particularly macroeconomic conditions that correlate with fuel prices. Stronger causal designs, such as regression discontinuity approaches exploiting state-level fuel tax changes or natural experiments from supply disruptions, would strengthen the evidence base.

Third, the threshold estimates identified in the literature are sensitive to specification choices, time period, and population. The convergence around $3.50–$4.50/gallon (2010–2015 dollars) is suggestive but should not be interpreted as a precise, universal threshold. Future research should examine how thresholds vary with local economic conditions, vehicle fleet fuel efficiency, and the availability of alternative care access points.

Fourth, this review has not examined the effect of electric vehicle adoption on the fuel price–ED utilization relationship. As electric vehicles become more prevalent, the fuel price mechanism will apply to a decreasing share of the driving population. However, in the near term, EV adoption rates are lowest among the low-income and rural populations most affected by fuel price barriers, meaning the relationship reviewed here is likely to persist for the most vulnerable populations even as it attenuates for the population overall.

Future research priorities include: (1) updated analyses incorporating post-2020 fuel price data, which includes both the COVID-era price collapse and the 2022 price spike; (2) studies examining the interaction between fuel prices and telehealth availability in moderating ED utilization effects; (3) natural experiment designs exploiting fuel tax policy changes for stronger causal identification; (4) patient-level studies that directly survey transportation cost as a factor in ED care-seeking decisions; and (5) evaluation of transportation assistance interventions specifically designed for emergency care access.

## 10. Conclusion

The evidence reviewed in this paper supports the conclusion that gasoline prices do affect emergency department utilization, but in a manner that is non-linear, population-specific, and concentrated in lower-acuity presentations. The relationship is strongest among rural, low-income, and uninsured populations—those for whom fuel cost represents a larger share of household budget and for whom driving distances to the ED are longest. High-acuity emergencies show minimal price sensitivity, consistent with the theoretical expectation that demand for truly emergent care is highly inelastic.

The threshold evidence, while subject to important caveats, converges around the $3.50–$4.50 per gallon range (in 2010–2015 dollars) as a zone within which fuel cost transitions from a non-salient background expense to a material factor in care-seeking decisions for price-sensitive populations. Below this range, fuel price variation has limited effect on ED volumes; above it, non-urgent and semi-urgent visits decline measurably, with effects of 3–12% depending on population and setting.

The most important implication of this evidence is that gasoline prices function as a regressive structural barrier to emergency care—one that is invisible in the design of the healthcare system but consequential in its operation. Unlike insurance barriers, which are the subject of extensive policy attention, transportation cost barriers operate outside the healthcare system's institutional boundaries and are rarely addressed by health policy. Yet for a patient who cannot afford the fuel to drive to the ED, the practical effect is the same as lacking insurance: the formal right to care does not translate into actual access to care.

Health systems and policymakers should incorporate transportation cost into their understanding of emergency care access, invest in transportation assistance programs designed for unscheduled emergency visits, expand telehealth triage capacity as a partial buffer, and include fuel cost in the formulas used to identify medically underserved communities. The goal is not to make ED visits insensitive to all costs—some reduction in non-urgent ED utilization may be clinically appropriate—but to ensure that the cost barrier of fuel does not prevent patients with genuine emergency needs from reaching care, and that the burden of transportation cost does not fall disproportionately on populations already facing multiple barriers to health.
