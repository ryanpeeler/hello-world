# Simulated Peer Review — Patient-Provider Racial and Ethnic Concordance Literature Review

## Reviewer 1

**Overall Assessment:** Accept with minor revisions

**Strengths:**
- Comprehensive synthesis of the concordance literature across four well-defined outcome domains
- Appropriate inclusion of landmark studies (Cooper et al. 2003, Alsan et al. 2019, Greenwood et al. 2020)
- Strong theoretical grounding in the implicit bias and medical mistrust literatures
- Actionable policy recommendations supported by evidence

**Weaknesses:**
- The section on diagnostic accuracy relies heavily on indirect evidence from the implicit bias literature; the review would benefit from acknowledging this evidentiary gap more explicitly
- Limited discussion of concordance effects in specialty care settings (e.g., surgery, psychiatry, obstetrics)
- The workforce section could benefit from updated pipeline data on Black medical student enrollment trends

**Minor Comments:**
- Consider adding a PRISMA flow diagram to document the systematic search and screening process
- The abstract could include a sentence on the number of studies included
- Table summarizing key study characteristics (design, sample size, setting, primary findings) would enhance readability

## Reviewer 2

**Overall Assessment:** Accept with minor revisions

**Strengths:**
- Well-organized thematic synthesis that appropriately distinguishes between subjective and objective outcomes
- Inclusion of both observational and experimental evidence strengthens causal inference
- Thoughtful discussion of mechanisms, including the role of historical mistrust (Tuskegee)
- Balanced assessment of limitations

**Weaknesses:**
- Would benefit from quantitative summary measures where possible (e.g., pooled effect sizes for patient satisfaction)
- The treatment adherence section could explore differential effects by disease severity and treatment complexity in more depth
- Missing discussion of intersectionality — how do gender, socioeconomic status, and age interact with racial concordance effects?
- Some citations appear to be from the early 2000s; additional recent literature (2020-2025) would strengthen currency

**Minor Comments:**
- Greenwood et al. 2020 finding of 58% mortality reduction merits careful contextualization regarding absolute vs. relative risk
- Consider discussing telehealth and whether concordance effects operate differently in virtual care settings

## Reviewer 3

**Overall Assessment:** Accept

**Strengths:**
- This is a needed and timely synthesis of the concordance literature
- The review correctly identifies that concordance effects are most pronounced for subjective outcomes and communication domains
- The Alsan et al. 2019 RCT is appropriately highlighted as the strongest causal evidence
- Policy recommendations are practical and evidence-based

**Suggestions:**
- A conceptual framework figure would help readers understand the hypothesized causal pathways
- Discussion of quality improvement interventions that have successfully narrowed concordance-related disparities would be valuable
- Brief mention of international perspectives on concordance would provide useful context
