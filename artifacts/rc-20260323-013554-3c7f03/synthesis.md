# Literature Synthesis

## Key Findings

The evidence on patient-provider racial concordance among Black patients converges on several consistent themes:

1. **Communication quality is the most robust concordance outcome.** Race-concordant visits are longer, feature more patient-centered communication, and are rated more positively by patients (Cooper et al. 2003, Shen et al. 2018).

2. **Patient satisfaction and trust show large concordance effects.** Black patients with concordant physicians report significantly greater satisfaction (OR 2.36, LaVeist & Nuru-Jeter 2002) and trust comparable to white patients with white physicians.

3. **Treatment adherence effects are context-dependent.** Concordance improves cardiovascular medication adherence (Traylor et al. 2010) and accelerates HIV treatment initiation (King et al. 2004), but effects are not uniform across all disease categories.

4. **Hard outcomes show compelling effects in large studies.** Greenwood et al. (2020) found a 58% reduction in the Black newborn mortality penalty with concordant physicians, and Alsan et al. (2019) found 63% increases in preventive service uptake in an RCT.

5. **Implicit bias and historical mistrust are key mechanisms.** The majority of providers hold implicit pro-white/anti-Black bias (Hall et al. 2015), and medical mistrust rooted in historical mistreatment remains a barrier to care for Black patients (Gamble 1997).

6. **Workforce underrepresentation constrains concordance availability.** Black physicians comprise approximately 5% of the physician workforce but are disproportionately responsible for care of Black communities (Komaromy et al. 1996, Bach et al. 2004).
