# Peer Review: Beyond Cultural Competency Training

**Manuscript Title:** Beyond Cultural Competency Training: Cultural Fluency as Structural Clinical Infrastructure and Its Effects on Diagnostic Accuracy, Patient Trust, Treatment Adherence, and Health Disparities in Black and Minority Patient Populations in the United States

**Journal:** [Research Pipeline Peer Review]
**Review Date:** 2026-03-23
**Decision:** Accept with Minor Revisions (Reviewers 1 & 2); Accept (Reviewer 3)

---

## Reviewer 1

**Expertise:** Health disparities research; cultural competency intervention design; clinical education

**Overall Assessment:** Accept with Minor Revisions

This manuscript makes a substantive contribution to the literature on clinical cultural training and structural health equity. The authors' central argument — that cultural fluency must be understood not merely as a trainable individual provider skill but as a feature of clinical infrastructure — is timely, well-framed, and persuasively developed. The paper surfaces an important and undertheorized tension in the field: the gap between what individual-level cultural competency training can accomplish and what systems-level structural change is required to achieve. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions focused primarily on the empirical grounding of claims specific to cultural fluency (as distinct from cultural competency) outcomes.

**Strengths**

- The structural analysis is the clearest strength of the paper. The authors draw productively on the structural competency framework (Metzl & Hansen, 2014; Metzl & Kirmayer, 2018) to argue that clinical cultural fluency cannot be reduced to individual provider behavior, and the synthesis of this literature with health disparities data is executed with notable precision.
- The paper's framing of cultural fluency as infrastructure — rather than as a curriculum module or training outcome — is a conceptual advance that distinguishes this work from prior systematic reviews of cultural competency interventions. This framing has implications for how healthcare institutions allocate resources and design accountability mechanisms.
- The review of mechanisms linking provider cultural behavior to diagnostic accuracy outcomes is thorough. The discussion of racial bias in pain assessment (Hoffman et al., 2016), implicit attitudes in clinical communication (Cooper et al., 2006), and physician-patient racial concordance effects (Greenwood et al., 2020; Alsan et al., 2019) is well-integrated and clearly supports the structural argument.
- The paper handles the medical mistrust literature carefully, contextualizing it within historical and ongoing structural conditions (Gamble, 1997; Williamson et al., 2019; Boulware et al., 2003) rather than treating mistrust as a patient-side deficit to be overcome by individual provider charm.
- The treatment adherence section is well-executed. The synthesis connecting patient-provider concordance, communication quality, and adherence outcomes (Traylor et al., 2010; Saha et al., 1999; Cooper et al., 2003) is coherent and appropriately qualified.

**Weaknesses**

- The paper's most significant empirical gap is the limited evidence base cited specifically for cultural fluency training outcomes, as distinguished from cultural competency training outcomes. The systematic reviews cited (Beach et al., 2005; Truong et al., 2014; Lie et al., 2011) are largely evaluations of cultural competency interventions. The authors argue that cultural fluency is a distinct and more structurally embedded construct, but the empirical support for differential outcomes between these approaches is underdeveloped. The authors should either present the existing evidence bearing on this distinction more explicitly or more clearly acknowledge it as a gap the paper is calling attention to.
- The paper would benefit from a more direct engagement with implementation research on structural interventions. The argument that cultural fluency operates as infrastructure is compelling theoretically, but the review does not substantially address what structural implementation looks like in practice or what evidence exists for infrastructure-level interventions at the health system level.
- The discussion of physician training pipeline diversity (Saha et al., 2008; Bach et al., 2004) is relevant but somewhat disconnected from the structural fluency argument as developed elsewhere in the paper. The relationship between workforce diversity and institutional cultural fluency capacity deserves a more explicit theoretical bridge.
- The paper's scope is broad — covering diagnostic accuracy, patient trust, treatment adherence, and health disparities — and in places the integration across these domains feels insufficiently tight. A clearer organizing logic for how structural cultural fluency is expected to operate differently across these outcome domains would strengthen the manuscript.

**Minor Comments**

- The term "cultural fluency" is used somewhat variably across sections — at times as a provider-level attribute, at other times as a system-level property. A terminological anchor early in the paper would help readers track the argument across sections.
- The paper's conclusion calls for "restructuring clinical infrastructure around cultural fluency" without specifying what existing structural models (e.g., patient-centered medical home, community health worker integration, language access programs) might be built upon. Even brief reference to existing structural exemplars would make the prescriptive section more actionable.
- Minor formatting note: citation style is inconsistent in sections 3 and 5 — a final proofread against the reference list is recommended.

## Reviewer 2

**Expertise:** Social determinants of health; intersectionality in clinical research; telehealth equity

**Overall Assessment:** Accept with Minor Revisions

This is a well-executed literature review that addresses a genuinely important gap. The paper's core contribution — the distinction between individual-level cultural competency training and structural/systems-level cultural fluency — is handled with care and analytical depth. The review demonstrates command of the relevant literature across multiple clinical outcome domains and makes a case that is both empirically grounded and theoretically coherent. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the paper's underdevelopment of intersectional frameworks and its silence on emerging telehealth contexts.

**Strengths**

- The individual-structural tension analysis is the paper's central accomplishment, and the authors execute it well. The paper does not simply assert that structural factors matter; it traces the specific mechanisms by which the limits of individual-level training are expressed in clinical outcomes, drawing on implicit bias research (Hall et al., 2015; Penner et al., 2010; Van Ryn & Burke, 2000), communication quality data (Johnson et al., 2004; Fiscella et al., 2004), and disparities documentation (Smedley et al., 2003) in a way that builds a genuine argument rather than merely cataloguing findings.
- The paper's treatment of medical mistrust is analytically sophisticated. By situating mistrust within structural and historical conditions rather than presenting it as a free-floating attitudinal variable, the authors avoid the common error of treating patient-side responses to structural harm as the primary obstacle to care quality. The synthesis of Gamble (1997), Armstrong et al. (2013), and Boulware et al. (2003) is particularly effective here.
- The structural competency literature is integrated cleanly. The paper positions itself correctly within the Metzl-Hansen framework while also extending that framework toward specific clinical outcome domains that the structural competency literature has not always addressed directly.
- The review of patient-physician concordance research (Greenwood et al., 2020; Alsan et al., 2019; Saha et al., 1999) is appropriately nuanced, acknowledging that concordance effects are real and meaningful without collapsing into a claim that racial matching is a sufficient structural solution.
- The discussion of shared decision-making (Charles et al., 1997) within the structural fluency frame is a useful contribution. Situating SDM as a practice that structural fluency either enables or forecloses depending on systemic conditions adds genuine theoretical value.

**Weaknesses**

- The paper's treatment of race and minority identity is insufficiently intersectional. The review largely treats Black and minority patients as unified categories without examining how gender, disability status, socioeconomic class, immigration status, and sexuality intersect with race to produce heterogeneous experiences of structural clinical exclusion. The literature on structural racism and health (Petersen et al., 2019; Krieger, 2012) cited in the paper supports an intersectional expansion, and the failure to develop it is a missed opportunity that limits the paper's applicability to clinical practice.
- The paper does not address telehealth or digital health contexts at all. This is a significant omission given that telehealth expanded dramatically during and after the COVID-19 period and has introduced new structural dimensions of cultural fluency — including algorithmic bias, digital access disparities, and remote communication barriers — that are directly relevant to the paper's argument. The structural framing the authors develop would translate productively to telehealth contexts, and the omission makes the review feel dated.
- The paper's prescriptive sections (particularly in the conclusion) are less developed than its analytical sections. The authors make a compelling case for structural cultural fluency as a clinical imperative but offer relatively thin guidance on what structural change looks like across institution types (safety-net hospitals, rural clinics, federally qualified health centers). This asymmetry between diagnosis and prescription is a weakness that could be partially addressed with targeted additions.
- The review does not engage with the patient perspective literature as fully as it engages with the provider literature. The work on patient-reported disrespect (Blanchard & Lurie, 2004) and patient-centered communication (Cooper et al., 2003) is cited but not developed into a patient-voice strand that runs through the review.

**Minor Comments**

- The abstract does not adequately signal the structural/systems argument that is the paper's central contribution. A reader scanning only the abstract would come away with the impression that this is primarily a review of cultural competency training outcomes. Revision of the abstract to foreground the structural argument is strongly recommended.
- Section 4's discussion of treatment adherence would benefit from explicit acknowledgment of the adherence measurement literature's limitations — particularly the tendency to locate non-adherence in patient behavior while underattending to system-side barriers.
- The paper could usefully cite Neff et al. (2020) and the structural competency working group literature more prominently; the working group's practical frameworks are relevant to the implementation gap noted in the third weakness above.

## Reviewer 3

**Expertise:** Health equity policy; clinical systems redesign; quality improvement

**Overall Assessment:** Accept

This manuscript fills an important and underserved gap in the health disparities and clinical education literature. The field has produced a substantial body of work on cultural competency training as an individual-provider intervention, but comparatively little that rigorously examines the mismatch between individual-level training models and the structural conditions that produce health disparities in the first place. This paper addresses that gap directly and does so with appropriate scholarly rigor. I recommend acceptance.

**Strengths**

- The paper makes a clear and well-supported case that the dominant model of cultural competency — as a trainable provider skill assessed at the individual level — is structurally insufficient to address the mechanisms by which clinical disparities are produced and maintained. This argument is not entirely new, but this review is among the most thorough syntheses of the evidence bearing on it, and its framing of cultural fluency as clinical infrastructure rather than training outcome is a useful conceptual development.
- The scope of outcome domains covered — diagnostic accuracy, patient trust, treatment adherence, and health disparities broadly — is appropriate and well-handled. The paper demonstrates coherently that structural cultural fluency has implications across these domains in ways that individual training cannot fully reach, and the literature synthesis across sections is well-integrated.
- The paper's engagement with the racial concordance literature (Greenwood et al., 2020; Alsan et al., 2019; Bach et al., 2004) is particularly strong and reflects careful reading of a body of evidence that is sometimes oversimplified. The authors correctly note that concordance data reveals structural demand that the current physician workforce cannot meet, rather than simply prescribing matching as a solution.
- The historical grounding in the medical mistrust literature (Gamble, 1997) and its connection to contemporary mistrust measures (Williamson et al., 2019) is well-executed and positions the paper correctly within the longer arc of structural critique.
- The manuscript is clearly written, well-organized, and makes appropriate use of its evidence base throughout.

**Weaknesses**

- The paper would be strengthened considerably by incorporation of implementation cost-effectiveness data. The argument that cultural fluency should be treated as structural clinical infrastructure carries resource allocation implications that the paper does not address. Decision-makers at health systems level — the audience most positioned to act on the paper's recommendations — will need economic evidence. Even a brief section reviewing available data on cost-effectiveness of structural cultural interventions (e.g., language access programs, community health worker integration, diversity in training pipelines) relative to their downstream effects on outcomes and utilization would substantially increase the paper's policy relevance and persuasive reach.
- The paper does not adequately account for institutional variation. The structural argument is developed at a relatively high level of abstraction that does not distinguish between, for example, urban academic medical centers with existing DEI infrastructure and rural or resource-limited settings where the structural interventions being implied may face very different feasibility conditions. Some discussion of implementation heterogeneity would improve the paper's applicability.
- The conclusion, while appropriately ambitious, does not identify measurable structural indicators that would allow health systems to assess their cultural fluency infrastructure. Given that the paper argues for treating cultural fluency as infrastructure rather than training, some discussion of how such infrastructure would be assessed, audited, or compared across institutions is needed to make the argument practically operational.

**Minor Comments**

- The paper would benefit from citing the structural competency working group's practical implementation literature (Neff et al., 2020) in the methods and discussion sections, not only in the structural competency background section.
- A brief discussion of accreditation mechanisms — particularly how existing accreditation standards (LCME, ACGME, Joint Commission) do or do not currently operationalize structural cultural fluency requirements — would situate the paper's recommendations within the institutional landscape that governs implementation.
- A figure or table summarizing the outcome-domain evidence by mechanism type would aid readers in synthesizing the cross-domain argument; this is a suggestion rather than a requirement.
